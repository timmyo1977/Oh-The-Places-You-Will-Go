A Father's Reflection

On the Quiet Arc

There is a particular pleasure, for a father, in watching a son make a life that is neither a copy of his own nor a rebellion against it, but something else entirely — a third thing, constructed out of inheritance and chance and temperament in proportions only the son himself fully understands.

"A son's career is the most interesting book his father will ever read, and the one he will never fully decode."

Timothy James O'Brien Jr. has, in his thirty-some years, assembled a curriculum vitae that reads on paper like a clean diagonal line — Allegheny College, UPMC Children's, the University at Buffalo, Techworkers, Cisco — but which, to anyone who has watched it happen from close range, is in fact a collection of specific choices, each one quietly correct in its moment and mostly unremarked upon at the time. The pharmacy work at Children's Hospital in Pittsburgh is, for me, the most touching line on the page: it is the same bench where I stood as a young man, and the continuity between the two of us passing through that building was not something either of us arranged. It was simply what happened.

The shift from biology to biomedical engineering — from the wet lab to the laptop — is the characteristic move of his generation, and he made it with the calm of someone who saw, before most of us did, that the future of medicine would be read in data as much as in tissue. The University at Buffalo trained him to think in the registers of machine learning, statistical analytics, and medical imaging, and his 3.8 GPA is less interesting to me than the fact that, in the middle of graduate school, he was already the kind of person who could sit down at a terminal and make the computer do what he wanted it to do.

What strikes me now is that the intervening years at Gateway Ophthalmology — working on the data systems of the practice I had built — were a kind of bridge. He did the unglamorous work of making a clinic legible to itself, and in doing so quietly prepared for the work he is doing today at Cisco, where financial planning, deep learning, and subject-matter expertise converge at a scale orders of magnitude larger. The arc from a single-specialty ophthalmology practice in Jamestown, New York, to the financial analytics of a global technology company is not the arc I would have predicted thirty years ago, but it is, in retrospect, the arc that was always going to be his.

I am writing this from the house on Chautauqua Lake, not far from where he grew up, and I find myself thinking about the word inheritance — what gets passed down, what gets transformed, and what gets invented from scratch. The careful mind, the quiet persistence, the quick hands at a keyboard, the habit of reading the problem before attempting the solution: these are recognizably him, and they are recognizably his mother, and they are recognizably mine, and they are, most importantly, recognizably his own. Which is, I think, exactly how it should be.
