The Children  ·  A Life in Progress

Anna Christine, Today

The small girl who walked the shoreline at Playa del Carmen in 2005, footprints trailing behind her in the white Caribbean sand, is now a young woman with her own life, her own questions, her own stretches of unhurried morning.

This is her page — a place to mark who she is becoming, not who she was. The photographs change; the light is different; the sea is somewhere else entirely. But the habit of paying attention, of noticing the moment for what it is, seems to have carried through.

Some things you pass on without meaning to. This, I hope, is one of them.

Chronicle & Present Day

A Life in Full Motion

Where She Lives

Roanoke, Texas — the small North Texas town just north of Fort Worth that has quietly become one of the most interesting addresses in the Metroplex. A former cattle stop on the Chisholm Trail, it is now known as the "Unique Dining Capital of Texas," with a walkable Oak Street lined with independent restaurants, and sits adjacent to the vast Alliance Texas development that anchors her professional life.

Work & Vocation

Marketing at Hillwood Properties, the Perot family's real estate and development firm, whose portfolio includes AllianceTexas — one of the largest master-planned developments in the country. She brings to the role the storytelling training of Syracuse University's S.I. Newhouse School of Public Communications, one of the country's premier programs in communications and media.

How She Got Here

Before Hillwood, Anna Christine spent several years in Los Angeles working as a producer on The Dr. Phil Show, the long-running nationally syndicated daytime program. Producing that kind of television is fast, demanding work — booking guests, shaping segments, managing sensitive stories under a daily deadline — and she built a reputation for handling it with poise. It is the foundation the Hillwood role is built on.

Recent Travels

The Los Angeles years gave her the West Coast, and the move to Texas has opened up the Southwest in a new way. Trips back to Western New York to see family, the occasional return to Playa del Carmen, and the slow accumulation of places seen with Jackson make up the current map.

People & Community

Her partner is Jackson Oberg. Together they have built a life in the Metroplex that centers on the people closest to them — a small circle of family, friends, and colleagues who have followed her from Syracuse to Los Angeles to North Texas, and the new community she and Jackson are building in Roanoke.

What's Ahead

A career in marketing at Hillwood has a long runway, and North Texas is growing faster than almost anywhere else in the country. Beyond the work, the next chapter is the one she and Jackson are writing together — which, as these things go, is best left to them to tell.

"Children grow up in two directions at once: forward into the people they will be, and backward into the memory of the people we knew them as."

There is a photograph in this collection, taken in 2005 on a beach in Mexico, of a very small girl holding her father's hand. Twenty years on, the girl and the father are both still here, and both still occasionally walk the edge of some body of water in the early morning. But the girl is no longer small, and the father is no longer the one setting the pace of the walk.

That is the thing no one quite prepares you for — that children, if you are fortunate, do not disappear as they grow up. They simply become harder to keep up with. They develop opinions, itineraries, and an entire life's worth of footprints that do not require your company. The photograph on the other page is the one from before that transition. This page is for after.

What fills the sections above will change, and should. A page about a person in motion ought to move. The point is not to fix her in place but to mark the waypoints — to make a record, from a father's vantage, of a life that is recognizably continuous with the one on the beach at Playa del Carmen, and at the same time entirely her own.
