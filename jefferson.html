Commencement  ·  Jefferson Medical College  ·  Philadelphia, June 1991

Philadelphia  ·  1987–1991

Learning to Heal

Medical school is not a place so much as a passage — a compressed, often brutal initiation into the science and art of human care. For four years, Jefferson Medical College in Philadelphia was the crucible in which that passage was made.

The photograph above is from the other side of that passage: commencement, June 1991. Academic robe with the green hood of medicine. Nancy at my side. Baby Timmy — Timothy Jr. — eight months old, held aloft in a sailor suit, a small sailor launched alongside a newly minted physician. We had arrived in Philadelphia as a young couple in 1987 and were leaving it as a family of three, with an MD in hand and a residency at Wills Eye Hospital about to begin.

Jefferson, founded in 1824, is among the oldest and most storied medical schools in America. To understand the institution is to understand something essential about how American medicine came to be.

The Moment Things Changed

Some pieces of paper change a life. This one — a small, cream-colored card from Philadelphia, embossed with Jefferson's seal in gold — was one of them.

The admission was through Jefferson's Physician Shortage Area Program, created to bring physicians back to underserved regions of Pennsylvania and the rural Northeast. A first-generation college student from Bradford, Pennsylvania was exactly the kind of candidate the program was designed for. The welcome was signed by the Associate Dean for Admissions in his own hand.

Years earlier, at Pitt-Bradford, a pre-pharmacy advisor named Dr. Thomas Gerteisen had told me I should go straight to medical school. I couldn't afford to take that advice at the time, so I finished pharmacy at Pitt, worked at Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh, and only then — armed with a profession, a wife, and some savings — applied to medicine.

The card is taped into an old scrapbook now, edges yellowing, next to a grainy photograph of Jefferson's front doors on Tenth Street. Looking at it, I remember opening the envelope in the mail at the kitchen table and not quite believing what it said.

Institution & Legacy

Jefferson Medical College & Its Deep Past

Founded 1824

Jefferson Medical College was established in Philadelphia on January 19, 1824 — the same year that Beethoven completed his Ninth Symphony and Byron died at Missolonghi. It was chartered by the Pennsylvania General Assembly as an independent medical school, distinct from the University of Pennsylvania, and quickly became one of the most attended medical colleges in the nation. For much of the 19th century, it graduated more physicians than any other school in the world.

The Gross Clinic

In 1875, Thomas Eakins painted The Gross Clinic — a monumental canvas depicting Samuel D. Gross, Jefferson's most celebrated surgeon, performing an operation in the Jefferson amphitheater surrounded by students. The painting was called the greatest American painting of the 19th century by many critics, and it captures something essential about Jefferson's ethos: medicine practiced with unflinching rigor, observed, taught, and witnessed. The original hangs today at the Philadelphia Museum of Art.

Samuel D. Gross

Gross — the "Emperor of American Surgery" — defined Jefferson's reputation across the mid-1800s. A towering figure in surgical education, his System of Surgery was the defining textbook of the era. He was among the first in America to teach surgery as a clinical science rather than a craft guild mystery, insisting that students observe operations directly rather than merely read about them. Jefferson's tradition of clinical teaching flows directly from his example.

Philadelphia & American Medicine

Philadelphia has a legitimate claim to being the birthplace of American medicine. Pennsylvania Hospital, founded in 1751 by Benjamin Franklin and Dr. Thomas Bond, was the first hospital in the colonies. The College of Physicians of Philadelphia — home of the Mütter Museum — was founded in 1787. Jefferson entered this environment in 1824 as a brash, independent alternative, and its graduates fanned out across a growing nation carrying its clinical philosophy with them.

Wills Eye & the Philadelphia Connection

Philadelphia was also home to Wills Eye Hospital, founded in 1832 as the first eye hospital in the Western Hemisphere. The connection between Jefferson and Wills Eye would become a defining thread in Tim O'Brien's own story — from medical school through residency and into a career in ophthalmology. The city's medical ecosystem made such trajectories possible: world-class institutions within blocks of one another, sharing faculty, patients, and ideas across two centuries.

Thomas Jefferson University Today

In 2017, Jefferson Medical College merged with Philadelphia University to form Thomas Jefferson University — a comprehensive institution spanning medicine, design, health, and the built environment. The medical school, now Sidney Kimmel Medical College at Thomas Jefferson University, retains Jefferson's name and legacy while operating as part of one of the largest academic health systems in the Philadelphia region. It continues to graduate physicians in the tradition established two centuries ago.

"Medicine is learned by the bedside and not in the classroom." — Sir William Osler

Jefferson's founding philosophy was Oslerian before Osler articulated it. From its earliest decades, the college insisted that clinical experience — standing at the patient's side, in the amphitheater, in the ward — was the irreplaceable core of medical education. This was not a given in 1824. Much of American medical education at the time was delivered by lecture alone, without systematic access to patients. Jefferson's insistence on clinical contact was radical, and it shaped a generation of American physicians who approached the human body as something to be observed carefully, not merely categorized.

The four years of medical school trace a recognizable arc. The first two are largely preclinical: anatomy, biochemistry, physiology, pharmacology, pathology — the fundamental sciences that make the body legible. The second two are clinical: rotations through internal medicine, surgery, psychiatry, pediatrics, obstetrics, and the specialties. The commencement photograph at the top of this page was taken at the end of all of it, in the small courtyard outside the Jefferson amphitheater where Eakins had once sketched Gross at work. By then the academic hood signified something earned rather than aspired to, and Timmy was along for the ride — a small passenger through the last semester, arrived into the world that fall.

Philadelphia in the late 1980s was a particular kind of education in itself. The city's medical quarter — Jefferson, Thomas Jefferson University Hospital, Wills Eye, Pennsylvania Hospital, and Jefferson's affiliated clinics — formed a dense constellation of medical experience. To rotate through those institutions as a student was to encounter pathology in its full range: the acute and the chronic, the urban and the suburban, the insured and the uninsured. The city did not shelter its medical students from complexity. It delivered complexity, at volume, without apology.

What Jefferson produced, at its best, was not merely a credentialed physician but a certain kind of medical citizen: someone formed by the Gross tradition of watching carefully, by the Philadelphia tradition of taking medicine seriously as both science and service, and by the particular discipline of having been trained in one of the oldest and most demanding medical environments in the country. The acceptance card and the commencement photograph bookend that formation — four years, two documents, one passage. Everything that came after — Wills Eye, Jamestown, Gateway Ophthalmology, thirty-five years of cataract surgery — traces back to the piece of cream-colored cardstock that arrived in the mail one afternoon in Pittsburgh, and to the quiet confidence of a young man in a green hood holding his first son up to the sun.
